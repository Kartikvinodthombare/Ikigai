Understanding Ikigai
Foundation

What Is Ikigai?

Your reason for being — the happiness of always being busy

The word ikigai is written in Japanese as 生き甲斐 — combining 生き (life) with 甲斐 (to be worthwhile). The character 甲 means "armour," "number one," and "to be the first to head into battle." The character 斐 means "beautiful" or "elegant." Together, ikigai translates roughly as "the happiness of always being busy."

According to those born on Okinawa — the island with the most centenarians in the world — ikigai is the reason we get up in the morning. Everyone has an ikigai, but finding it requires a patient search. It is hidden deep inside each of us.

The 4 Universal Keys

According to scientists who have studied all five Blue Zones, the keys to longevity are identical across every region:

Diet

Predominantly plant-based, low in processed foods and salt, moderate in calories. Little meat. Alcohol, if at all, in moderation and always with food or friends.

Exercise

Not strenuous or gym-based. Daily low-intensity movement: walking, gardening, cycling. People in Blue Zones walk rather than drive whenever possible.

Purpose (Ikigai)

A clearly defined reason for living that is just as important as diet or exercise — perhaps even more so. Without purpose, health habits alone are insufficient.

Social Ties

A broad circle of friends and strong family relations. Members of these communities manage time well, reduce stress, and are embedded in supportive networks.

友
The Moai Practice (Implement This Week): Identify 3–5 people with a shared interest. Commit to a monthly meeting — dinner, a game, a walk. Make a small monthly contribution that goes toward group activities. This is the Okinawan moai system and its function is emotional, financial, and social stability. The feeling of belonging and support directly increases life expectancy.

Anti-Aging Secrets
Longevity Science

Little Things That
Add Up to a Long Life

Stress, mental exercise, movement, sleep — the science and the action

Mental Exercise

  • Our neurons start ageing in our twenties — but intellectual activity slows this process
  • The brain needs new information to create new connections and avoid ingrained habits (autopilot)
  • Learning something new every day is an essential antiaging strategy
  • Playing games and interacting with others prevents depression from solitude
  • A more positive outlook yields greater mental benefit
  • Action: Learn one new skill. Play strategy games. Interact socially daily.

Stress Management

  • Sustained stress promotes cellular ageing by weakening telomeres
  • The greater the stress, the greater the degenerative effect on cells
  • Low-level stress is beneficial — it drives us to develop healthier habits
  • High, sustained stress damages healthy cells and causes depression
  • Prevention through mindfulness is more effective than treatment
  • Action: Practice mindfulness, meditation, yoga, or breathing exercises daily
Beating Sedentary Behaviour

A lack of physical activity progressively distorts telomeres in the immune system, which ages those cells — and in turn, the entire organism. Spending too much time seated reduces muscular fitness, increases appetite, and curbs desire to participate in activities.

  • Walk to work or go for a walk of at least 20 minutes every day
  • Use stairs instead of elevators or escalators — good for posture, muscles and respiratory system
  • Participate in social or leisure activities so you don't spend too much time in front of screens
  • Replace junk food with fruit to reduce snacking urges while adding nutrients
  • Sleep 7–9 hours — any more makes us lethargic; any less accelerates ageing
  • Play with children, pets, or join a team — strengthens body and stimulates mind
The Sleep Advantage

When we sleep, we generate melatonin — a powerful antioxidant that helps us live longer. Melatonin strengthens the immune system, contains elements that protect against cancer, promotes insulin production, slows Alzheimer's onset, and helps prevent osteoporosis. Melatonin production decreases after age 30. Counteract this by:

Eat Balanced + Calcium

A balanced diet with adequate calcium supports melatonin production as natural levels decline with age

Daily Moderate Sun

Soak up a moderate amount of sunlight each day to regulate your circadian rhythm and support production

Prioritise Sleep

Get consistent, adequate sleep. 7–9 hours, same schedule. This is the single most powerful antiaging tool

Avoid Disruptors

Stress, alcohol, tobacco, and caffeine all make it harder to get a good night's rest, depriving you of melatonin

Finding Your Purpose
Logotherapy & Morita Therapy

From Empty to Purposeful

Viktor Frankl's process + Shoma Morita's method — combined into action

Logotherapy — developed by Viktor Frankl — helps people find their purpose in life. According to Frankl: "He who has a why to live for can bear with almost any how." Logotherapy pushes people to consciously discover their life's purpose in order to confront their neuroses — their quest to fulfil their destiny then motivates them forward, breaking mental chains of the past.

Viktor Frankl's 5-Step Process
1

Feel the Emptiness Without Judgement

A person feels empty, frustrated, or anxious. Logotherapy does not see this as mental illness — it is spiritual anguish, a natural and beneficial phenomenon that drives you to seek meaning.

2

Name It — The Desire for a Meaningful Life

Recognise that what you feel is the desire to have a meaningful life. This reframing turns anxiety into fuel rather than paralysis.

3

Discover Your Life's Purpose (Right Now)

Find your purpose at this particular point in time. Your ikigai is not permanent — it can be adjusted or transformed many times over the years. Start with what is meaningful now.

4

Choose It Freely

Of your own free will, decide to accept or reject that destiny. The decision must be yours — not imposed by family, culture, or money.

5

Let Purpose Carry You Forward

This newfound passion for life helps you overcome obstacles and sorrows. What we need is not a peaceful existence but a meaningful challenge we can strive to meet using all the skills at our disposal.

Morita Therapy's 3 Core Principles

Accept Your Feelings

Do not try to control or eliminate obsessive thoughts — this makes them more intense. Morita likened emotions to weather: observe them without getting carried away. Welcome solitude, anxiety, fear as visitors.

Do What You Should Be Doing

Focus on the present moment. Avoid intellectualising. Character is built through what we do. Recovery comes on its own when we focus on action, not on eliminating symptoms.

Naikan Reflection

Each day, ask yourself three questions about someone important: (1) What have I received from them? (2) What have I given them? (3) What problems have I caused them? This deepens responsibility and reduces blame.

Discover Your Life's Purpose

Ask constantly: "What do I need to be doing right now? What action should I be taking?" Dare to look inside yourself. Find your ikigai — your existential fuel.

Achieving Flow
Csikszentmihalyi's Research

Turn Work Into Flow

The optimal experience — and how to reliably reach it

Psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi defined flow as "the state in which people are so involved in an activity that nothing else seems to matter; the experience itself is so enjoyable that people will do it even at great cost, for the sheer sake of doing it." Flow is the same across all ages and cultures. A chess player, a mathematician, and a surgeon all reach the same state of flow in the same way.

The Flow Spectrum
Too Easy
Boredom
Skills exceed the challenge. Mind wanders. Apathy sets in.
Optimal Zone
Flow
Challenge matches skill with slight stretch. Time disappears. Ego dissolves.
Too Hard
Anxiety
Challenge exceeds skills. Frustration. Likely to give up.
Owen Schaffer's 7 Conditions for Flow
  • Know what to do — clear objective before starting
  • Know how to do it — you have sufficient skill to attempt the task
  • Know how well you are doing — feedback is immediate
  • Know where to go when navigation is involved
  • Perceive significant challenges — the task must stretch you
  • Perceive significant skills — you believe you can complete it
  • Be free from distractions — single-focused environment
3 Strategies to Reach Flow
1

Choose a Difficult Task — But Not Too Difficult

The ideal is a middle path: aligned with your abilities but just a stretch. If you're a graphic designer, learn a new software program. If you're a programmer, use a new language. Add a little something extra that takes you out of your comfort zone. As Hemingway said, "Sometimes I write better than I can."

2

Have a Clear, Concrete Objective — Then Focus on Process

Set a specific objective before you begin. Then let it go when you sit down to work. A compass pointing to a concrete objective is more useful than a detailed map. Once the journey begins, keep the objective in mind without obsessing over it. As Einstein said, "A happy man is too satisfied with the present to dwell on the future."

3

Concentrate on a Single Task

Multitasking lowers productivity by at least 60% and IQ by over 10 points. Concentrating on one thing at a time may be the single most important factor in achieving flow. Scientific evidence shows combining tasks has the opposite of the intended effect — even people who think they are good at multitasking are among the least productive.

Your Anti-Distraction Protocol (Implement Tomorrow Morning)

Do not look at any screen for the first hour after waking. Turn your phone off before a focus session. Try the Pomodoro Technique: 25 minutes of focused work, then 5 minutes of rest. Start your session with a ritual you enjoy; end with a reward. Work in a space where you will not be interrupted. Bundle routine tasks — invoices, calls, emails — into one dedicated window. Happiness is in the doing, not in the result. "Rituals over goals."

Lessons from Centenarians
100 Interviews in Ogimi

What the Longest-Living
People Actually Do

Distilled from one hundred interviews with Ogimi's eldest residents

The authors interviewed one hundred of the eldest residents in Ogimi — the Village of Longevity — asking about their life philosophy, their ikigai, and the secrets to longevity. Five recurring themes emerged across all interviews.

Don't Worry

"The secret to a long life is not to worry. And to keep your heart young — don't let it grow old. Open your heart to people with a nice smile on your face." The best way to avoid anxiety: go out into the street and say hello to people every day. Spend time with friends. "Here, everyone gets along. We try not to cause problems. Spending time together and having fun is the only thing that matters."

Cultivate Good Habits

Wake up at the same time. Tend a vegetable garden. Do taiso exercises each morning. "I feel joy every morning waking up at six and opening the curtains to look at my garden." "The key to staying sharp in old age is in your fingers — from your fingers to your brain, and back again." "Working. If you don't work, your body breaks down."

Nurture Friendships Every Day

"Getting together with my friends is my most important ikigai." "Talking each day with the people you love, that's the secret to a long life." "I wake up at five every morning, leave the house, and walk to the sea. Then I go to a friend's house and we have tea together. That's the secret to long life: getting together with people, and going from place to place."

Live an Unhurried Life

"My secret to a long life is always saying to myself, 'Slow down,' and 'Relax.' You live much longer if you're not in a hurry." "Doing many different things every day. Always staying busy, but doing one thing at a time, without getting overwhelmed." "The secret to long life is going to bed early, waking up early, and going for a walk. Living peacefully and enjoying the little things."

Be Optimistic

"Every day I say to myself, 'Today will be full of health and energy. Live it to the fullest.'" "I'm ninety-eight, but consider myself young. I still have so much to do." "Laugh. Laughter is the most important thing. I laugh wherever I go." "The most important thing in Ogimi, in life, is to keep smiling." "There's no secret to it. The trick is just to live."

祝
The Ogimi Keys (from the interviews): 100% of seniors keep a vegetable garden. All belong to a neighbourhood association that feels like family. They celebrate constantly — music, song, and dance are daily life. They have an important purpose but don't take it too seriously. They are passionate about everything they do, however insignificant it may seem. They are always busy, but with tasks that allow them to relax.

Hara Hachi Bu — The 80% Rule (Practice Starting Today)

Before every meal, remind yourself: eat until you are 80% full — not completely full. When you notice you're almost full but could have a little more, stop. Skip dessert, or reduce portion size. Serve food on many small plates rather than one large plate — this naturally reduces how much you eat. The Japanese principle of hara hachi bu has been practised since at least the twelfth century and is directly tied to Okinawa's low caloric intake.

Core Dietary Principles from Okinawan Research
  • Eat a wide variety of foods — at least 18 different foods per day, across as many colours as possible ("eat the rainbow")
  • Eat at least 5 servings of fruits and vegetables every day, including at least 7 types
  • Grains are the foundation — white rice, noodles. More than 30% of calories from vegetables
  • Rarely eat sugar — if you do, use cane sugar. Okinawans consume one-third as much sugar as the rest of Japan
  • Eat fish three times per week. Pork and other meat once or twice per week at most
  • Eat from many small plates rather than large servings — this facilitates variety and prevents overeating

Green / White Tea — Daily Non-Negotiable

緑茶 · 白茶

Okinawans drink an average of 3 cups of Sanpin-cha (green + jasmine) per day. Green tea controls cholesterol, lowers blood sugar, improves circulation, and provides protection against flu and UV damage. White tea has the highest antioxidant concentration of any natural product — one cup may equal approximately a dozen glasses of orange juice. Drinking green or white tea daily reduces free radicals and slows cellular ageing.

Gentle Movements
Eastern Disciplines

Move Every Day,
Live Longer

The people who live longest are not those who exercise most — but those who move most

Studies from the Blue Zones confirm that residents walk a lot, garden, do karaoke, and get up early. They don't go to the gym or exercise intensely, but they almost never stop moving. Metabolism slows 90% after just 30 minutes of sitting. Enzymes that move fat from arteries to muscles for burning slow immediately. Good cholesterol drops 20% after two hours of sitting. Getting up for just five minutes restarts everything.

Radio Taiso

Japanese · Daily · 5–10 min
  • Done every morning by 30% of Japanese people
  • Practised by virtually everyone interviewed in Ogimi
  • Focuses on dynamic stretching and joint mobility
  • Raises arms above head — a movement rarely done in modern life
  • Promotes community spirit when done in groups
  • Do it: Search "radio taiso" on YouTube each morning

Tai Chi

Chinese · Daily · 20–30 min
  • Slows development of osteoporosis and Parkinson's disease
  • Increases circulation and improves muscle tone and flexibility
  • A great shield against stress and depression
  • Overcomes force through finesse — minimal effort, maximum effect
  • Key principle: "Look for stillness in movement"
  • Start with: Wave Hands Like Clouds — 10 minutes daily

Qigong

Chinese · Daily · 20 min
  • Modifies brain waves; improves balance of sex hormones
  • Lowers blood pressure in patients with hypertension
  • Greater bone density and better circulation
  • Decelerates symptoms associated with senility
  • Improves cardiac function; reduces cancer treatment side effects
  • Start with: The Five Elements series (Earth, Water, Wood, Metal, Fire)
Yoga — The Sun Salutation (12 Steps, Every Morning)

The Sun Salutation is one of the most iconic exercises in hatha yoga. It can be done by almost anyone and takes under 10 minutes. The sequence: (1) Stand straight, exhale. (2) Raise arms above head, bend back slightly, inhale. (3) Bend forward to touch the ground, exhale. (4) Stretch one leg back, inhale. (5) Both legs back, arms straight, hold breath. (6) Bring chest to ground, exhale. (7) Straighten arms, bend spine back, inhale. (8) Lift hips into inverted V, exhale. (9) Bring front foot forward, inhale. (10) Bring back foot forward, straighten legs, exhale. (11) Arms above head, bend back, inhale. (12) Lower arms, exhale. Repeat daily.

The Six Healing Sounds (Sun Simiao, 6th century)

六字訣 — Breathe · Move · Sound

Coordinate movement, breathing, and sound: Xu (like "shh" with a deep sigh) — associated with the liver. He (like "her" with a yawn) — the heart. Si (like "sir" with a slow exhale) — the lungs. Chui (like "chwee" with a forceful exhale) — the kidneys. Hoo (like "who") — the spleen. Xi (like "she") — connects the whole body. Practise each morning, one sound per breath cycle, to bring the body into calm alignment.

Resilience & Antifragility
Stoicism · Buddhism · Wabi-Sabi

Growing Stronger
Through Adversity

Resilience, wabi-sabi, and antifragility — the three-layer system

Stoic Practices

  • Know what you can control and what you cannot — focus only on the former
  • Practice negative visualisation: imagine the worst to prepare, without worrying about it
  • The goal is tranquility (apatheia): absence of anxiety, fear, shame, vanity, and anger
  • Seneca recommended negative visualisation every night before sleep
  • Deliberately live without certain comforts for a week to realise you can survive
  • "It's not what happens to you, but how you react that matters." — Epictetus

Buddhist Practices

  • Observe thoughts and emotions as they appear without getting carried away
  • Train the mind not to get swept up in anger, jealousy, or resentment
  • The only moment in which you can be truly alive is the present moment
  • Everything we have and all the people we love will disappear — accept this without pessimism
  • Awareness of impermanence should help us love the present and those who surround us
  • Use meditation daily to observe, not suppress, negative emotions
Wabi-Sabi + Ichi-go Ichi-e

Wabi-Sabi

侘び寂び

The beauty of the fleeting, changeable, and imperfect nature of the world. Instead of searching for beauty in perfection, look for it in things that are flawed, incomplete. Only things that are imperfect, incomplete, and ephemeral can truly be beautiful — because only those things resemble the natural world. Life is pure imperfection. Accept it with grace.

Ichi-go Ichi-e

一期一会

"This moment exists only now and won't come again." Each encounter — whether with friends, family, or strangers — is unique and will never be repeated. Enjoy the moment and do not lose yourself in worries about the past or future. Used in tea ceremonies, Zen meditation, and Japanese martial arts — all of which place emphasis on being fully present.

The 3 Steps to Antifragility (Nassim Taleb via the Book)
01

Create Redundancies

Don't rely on a single income source, relationship, or interest. Try to make money from hobbies or a secondary job. If you have multiple income streams and lose your primary job, your secondary becomes your main. Apply the same principle to friendships — cultivate strong relationships beyond one person. Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Every Ogimi senior had a primary and secondary occupation (usually a vegetable garden).

02

Bet Conservatively, Take Small Risks

Keep 90% of your resources safe (index funds, stable relationships, proven habits). Use the remaining 10% to take many small bets with high potential upside. You can lose some of those bets and still come out ahead if one pays off. The key is avoiding catastrophic downside while staying open to asymmetric upside. Never invest everything in one high-risk move.

03

Remove What Makes You Fragile

Instead of adding new challenges only, set "good riddance" goals: stop snacking between meals, pay off debt gradually, avoid toxic people, stop doing things you dislike out of obligation, limit social media to 20 minutes daily. Ask: "What makes me fragile?" Identify and remove those people, things, and habits systematically.

From the Wisdom of Ogimi's Long-Living Residents

The 10 Rules of Ikigai

Distilled by the authors from their week in Ogimi — implement all ten

1

Stay Active — Don't Retire

Those who give up what they love doing and do well lose their purpose. Keep doing things of value, making progress, bringing beauty or utility to others, even after "official" work has ended.

2

Take It Slow

Being in a hurry is inversely proportional to quality of life. "Walk slowly and you'll go far." When we leave urgency behind, life and time take on new meaning.

3

Don't Fill Your Stomach

Eat a little less than your hunger demands. Apply the 80% rule (hara hachi bu) at every meal. Less is more when it comes to eating for long life.

4

Surround Yourself with Good Friends

Friends are the best medicine — for confiding worries, sharing stories, getting advice, having fun, dreaming, living. Make time for them every single day.

5

Get in Shape for Your Next Birthday

The body you move through life needs daily maintenance. Exercise releases hormones that make us happy. Water moves; the body needs to move too.

6

Smile

A cheerful attitude is not only relaxing — it also helps make friends. Recognise what isn't great, but never forget what a privilege it is to be here, in a world full of possibilities.

7

Reconnect with Nature

Human beings are made to be part of the natural world. Return to it often to recharge. Even a daily walk outside or a vegetable garden counts.

8

Give Thanks

To your ancestors, to nature, to friends and family, to everything that brightens your days. Spend a moment every day giving thanks, and you'll watch your stockpile of happiness grow.

9

Live in the Moment

Stop regretting the past and fearing the future. Today is all you have. Make the most of it. Make it worth remembering. Ichi-go ichi-e.

10

Follow Your Ikigai

There is a passion inside you — a unique talent that gives meaning to your days and drives you to share the best of yourself until the very end. If you don't know it yet, your mission is to discover it.
